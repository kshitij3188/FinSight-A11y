Summary
This policy contains bunq’s approach to financial crime, and particularly money
laundering, terrorist financing and sanctions. It outlines: who are the responsible parties,
our risk appetite, how we classify risk, our onboarding process, the preventative measures
against money laundering and terrorist financing implemented by bunq.
Section 1 briefly introduces the policy, the legal basis for it;
Section 2 describes different people within bunq and their responsibilities;
Section 3 describes acceptance policy of bunq;
Section 4 describes how systemic risk analysis works at bunq;
Section 5 describes different steps of bunq risk classification system in accordance with
Dutch Law;
Section 6 sets out the onboarding process for different entities which want to become
bunq users;
Section 7 establishes a set of measures used to counteract money laundering which
bunq has in place;
Section 8 describes our approach towards sanctions of different countries and
organisation within the context of the policy;
Section 9 describes situation when bunq will offboard users;
Section 10 describes when bunq employees can make certain decisions different from
how the policy prescribes.

1. Introduction
To ensure compliance with anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorist financing
(CTF) laws and Sanctions regulations, bunq B.V. has developed and implemented
extensive measures such as policies, procedures, internal controls and systems.
This policy outlines bunqs application of relevant laws and regulations relating to
anti-money laundering and terrorism financing, specifically:
● The revised 4th European AML directive (Directive (EU) 2018/843), amending AML
Directive 2015/849;
● The Dutch Wet ter voorkoming van witwassen en financieren van terrorisme
(Wwft);
● Sections 3:10 and 3:17 of the Dutch Financial Supervision Act (Wft);
● Sections 10 and 14 of the Dutch Prudential Rules Decree (Bpr Wft);
● The Dutch Sanctions Act 1977 and sanction regulations issued by national and
international authorities (UN/EU/US/UK and NL);
● Guidance from the European Banking Authority, such as the EBA ML/TF Risk
Factors Guidelines under Articles 17 and 18(4) of EU Directive 2015/849;
● Ministry of Finance Guidance on the Anti-Money Laundering and Combating the
Terrorism Finance Act;
● DNB Guidance on the Anti-Money Laundering and Combating the Financing of
Terrorism Act and the Sanctions Act.
This policy has supporting procedural documents which describe the implementation of
this policy. This policy applies to all bunq departments and staff, including branches.
Subsidiaries can have their own risk management arrangements and are outside the
scope of this policy. However, risk oversight of the subsidiaries by (employees of) bunq is in
the scope of this policy. This policy can be complemented by annexes for local branches or
subsidiaries describing the local laws and regulations where they differ from that
described in this policy.

4. Systematic Integrity Risk Analysis
The Systematic Integrity Risk Analyses (SIRA) is an analysis of potential integrity risks and it
is central to bunq’s efforts to prevent money laundering and terrorist financing.
Integrity risks are defined by the Prudential Rules Decree (Bpr Wft) as "a threat to the
reputation, the capital or the results of a financial institution due to insufficient
compliance with the rules that are in force under or pursuant to the law. In our internal
SIRA we map this definition to our risk taxonomy which we refer to as Evas Wish List. You
can take a look at this List in our Risk Management Policy.
The SIRA answers the following questions:
● Which integrity risks does bunq face?
● Which (level of) risk are we willing to accept?

● How do we manage the integrity risks?
Based on the SIRA process, integrity risks are (i) identified, (ii) analyzed, (iii) managed
(mitigated) and (iv) monitored.
The analysis is maintained and reviewed by multiple groups:
● Chief Risk Officer (CRO): has the ultimate responsibility to perform the SIRA and
delegates the development of it to the Head of Compliance.
● The First line of defense (this line of defense is in direct connection with the users
and the company’s business objectives): manages (and mitigates) the identified
risks.
● Managing board: as mentioned above, the CRO holds the ultimate responsibility
for integrity risk analysis within the Managing Board.
● Supervisory board: discusses SIRA at least once a year and approves it.
This SIRA will be updated at least once a year and in case one of the following events
occurs:
● when a new geographical market is entered into;
● when a new product or service is launched;
● when a material change to an existing product or service is implemented;
● when our acceptance policy and/or risk appetite changes; and
● when a new integrity risk is identified;
● when there are relevant changes to the legislation; and
● when significant control deficiencies are identified e.g. from risk incidents, KPI
breaches, risk assessment, control testing activities or audits.
Relevant stakeholders have a monthly meeting to discuss whether one of the above
mentioned events will occur in the foreseeable future or has occurred in the past period,
and to update the SIRA accordingly.
Every quarter the CRO provides an updated integrity risk assessment in an aggregated
manner (at the Risk Driver level of the Eva Wish List, i.e. Level 3 of bunq risk taxonomy) to
the Risk and Audit Committee of the Supervisory Board. The report is provided in the form
of a Risk Heat Map with highlights followed by detailed updates from Risk and
Compliance departments.

Incident reporting
bunq does not tolerate any breach of the requirements set forth in this AML/CFT Policy
and related policies and procedures that could jeopardise the soundness and integrity of
our bank or damage the public trust and confidence in the financial system as a whole.
Any integrity incidents will be reported to DNB immediately and will be recorded in the
incident register that is held by Risk.
An incident is defined as any conduct or event that poses a serious threat to the integrity
of the business of bunq. To qualify as an incident, it does not matter who performs the
conduct or event. It may include conduct by staff members, directors, board members as

DNB is of the opinion that the duty to report an incident takes effect as soon as bunq is
aware of the incident. Thus, the results of our own investigation should not be awaited
before reporting the incident to DNB.
5. Risk Classification
In accordance with article 3 sub 8 of the Wwft, we adjust the customer's due diligence
measures based on the risk level of the customer. We follow this legal requirement by
using these risk models:
1. Onboarding risk-scoring models
a. A scoring model for each new user based on the information gathered
during the onboarding process (before account opening).
2. Dynamic risk scoring models
a. These models keep the risk score of each user up to date during their
relationship with bunq (after account opening).
Thus, there are 4 models in total, each with two versions (personal user and company
user).
The models take into account (if relevant):
● Risk factors that are mentioned in annexes to EU 5th AML Directive 2018/843;
● Analysis of historical data ;
● Users country and industry/sector risk ratings.

7. Measures to Counter Money Laundering
bunq has implemented a number of measures and processes in its anti-money
laundering efforts. These include country, industry, and legal form risk rankings to classify
different users
Country, industry, and legal form risk ranking
Important indicators for our onboarding, risk scoring, and monitoring rules are
I. the country of residence of the users and the UBOs;
II. the applicable company Standaard Bedrijfsindeling (SBI) codes risk rating2; and
III. the legal form of the user company.
These factors influence the measures bunq implements for screening, monitoring and
review of users.
Transaction monitoring
Transaction monitoring is the assessment of transactions for indications of money
laundering or terrorism financing to determine whether the transactions should be
reported as unusual to the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU). The purpose of transaction
monitoring is to identify user activities which appear to be unusual that should be further
examined and investigated. Additionally, all complex and unusually large transactions and
all unusual transaction patterns that have no clear economic or legal purpose are
examined.
A transaction is defined as an act or combination of acts by or on behalf of a customer of
which bunq has become aware with respect of its services to that customer. This can be
transactions from the user to a third party or from a third party to the user. This also
includes transactions or activities of the user with which bunq is confronted during the
provision of services. It also comprises activities and transactions related to UBOs, entities

in the structure of the user or other non-monetary transactions and activities. A causal
relationship between the transaction and the services of bunq is not required. A passive
involvement because bunq knows about a transaction in relation to bunq's services can
also trigger an alert.
To facilitate the transaction monitoring and analysis of alerts, a transaction profile based
on the peer-grouping is created for each user. The transaction profile is used to assess
whether the transactions that the user carries out are consistent with our knowledge of
the user and to flag any unusual behavior. A transaction profile is based on the transaction
behavior of a user and it is automatically updated.
We have developed a transaction monitoring system that consists of two parts:
pre-transaction monitoring (transaction filtering) and post-transaction monitoring.
Pre-transaction monitoring (pre-transaction filtering)
This process involves monitoring before a transaction is fully executed. In other words,
before a transaction reaches the account of the intended payee. This calls for several
monitoring rules, each with several criteria. When each criterion applies to a transaction or
transaction pattern, the rule applies, and an alert is created. When an alert is triggered,
the transaction gets automatically redirected to a suspense account. The redirection
occurs to prevent the transaction from reaching the payee.
All alerts show up as notifications in our customer relationship management system and
are investigated by our analysts. An alert investigated by our analysts who may reverse or
freeze/block the transaction if the investigation proves that the transaction is likely related
to any illegal activities.
Post-transaction monitoring
The post-transaction monitoring system refers to monitoring that takes place after a
transaction is fully executed. This system creates alerts in a similar fashion to the
pre-transaction monitoring system mentioned above, but it does not divert transactions,
because the monitoring takes place after the transactions have already been fully
executed. Most monitoring takes place post-execution in order not to disrupt payment
traffic too much (this is a common industry practice).
The post-transaction monitoring system consists of both monitoring rules and machine
learning models. The machine learning models have been trained on historical
14

## Page 16

transaction data we suspect to be related to money laundering or terrorism financing and
which we have detected in various ways over a period of several years.
The models are designed to detect whether a transaction or pattern is similar to a
transaction or a pattern the model was trained on. The output of the models is a similarity
score between zero and one. This score indicates how similar the model believes a
transaction is based on what it has learned from the historical data (0 - not similar at all, 1 -
exactly the same). In case the score is above a set threshold, an alert is created. The
threshold is the best possible balance between false positives and false negatives.
In some cases, the system will not only trigger an alert but also an automatic and
immediate account freeze or account restrictions. The account freeze or restrictions allow
an analyst to investigate a user without the risk that the user conducts (materially) more
illegal activities with his/her account in the course of the investigation.
Source of funds
Furthermore, besides transaction monitoring, we ask, where necessary, for a user's source
of funds. Our approach to company users is different from the approach to user persons
because the behavior of user companies is much more diverse and complex than that of
user persons. Our agents are tasked with manually assessing the behavior of user
companies and if needed manually inquiring about their source of funds.

Tax Integrity
As a bank, we have the regulatory obligation to take measures to ensure controlled and
sound business operations and to prevent involvement in financial and economic crime,
including money laundering related to tax evasion. We therefore investigate the
tax-driven motives of our business relations with respect to the services they wish to use
in order to determine whether we face potential tax evasion or tax avoidance related risks.
While tax avoidance is not illegal, its harmful effects could certainly damage our
reputation as well as the confidence in the Dutch financial sector. Tax avoidance and tax
evasion are jointly referred to as tax integrity risks. Tax integrity risks specifically occur in
cross-border transactions and among business relations with a complex international
company structure.
Part of CDD is therefore the evaluation of whether the user risk entails a higher tax
integrity risk. Where a (combination of) red flag(s) indicates a higher tax integrity risk,
bunq investigates the indicators to understand whether it is indicative of tax evasion or
aggressive tax avoidance and take appropriate measures to mitigate the tax risk(s)
identified.
Both the EU and the Netherlands have designated countries that are non-cooperative tax
jurisdictions. These lists are part of the efforts to fight tax evasion and avoidance and aim
to create a stronger deterrent for countries that consistently refuse to play fair on tax

Funds Transfers Regulation
The EU Regulation on information accompanying transfers of funds (Regulation (EU)
2015/847 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 20 May 2015 on information
accompanying transfers of funds) contains provisions on the traceability of transfers of
funds to prevent, detect and investigate money laundering and terrorism financing. The
aim of this regulation is to improve the traceability of information accompanying transfers
of funds. The Regulation obliges institutions to attach information to funds transfers and
to carry out checks on received transfers, so that information about the originator and the
beneficiary in the payment chain remains available and traceable.
The regulation applies to transfers of funds which are sent or received by a payment
service provider or an intermediary payment service provider established in the European
Union. The payment service provider of the payer is responsible for adequate recording of
information, while the payment service provider of the payee must check whether the
information received is complete.
bunq therefore monitors all transactions for missing originator and/or beneficiary
information. If the required information is missing or incomplete, we shall ask for the
required information on the originator and the beneficiary before crediting the user's
account. In case the required information remains missing, we will reject the transfer. Lack
of information or incomplete information on the originator or beneficiary when
transferring funds can be a reason for reporting an unusual transaction to the FIU.
FIU reporting
When we notice a possible unusual transaction, we make an independent assessment
whether the transaction is indeed unusual by way of the objective and subjective
indicators that are applicable under the Wwft.
Any unusual transaction - executed or not - must be reported immediately after the
unusual nature of the transaction has become known to us. The relevant subjective
indicator is a transaction in which the institution has reason to assume that it may be
related to money laundering or financing terrorist. If and insofar it is established that a
transaction is unusual, we will promptly file a report with the FIU.

Reporting of Objective 6 of the Implementation Decree of Wwft
Objective 6 of the Implementation Decree of Wwft refers to the obligation of Banks to
report to FIU. The use of a credit card or a prepaid payment instrument (prepaid card) in
connection with a transaction for an amount of 15,000 or more.
This article refers specifically to credit card transactions, involving a lender and a borrower.
From the definition of credit transaction by the NL Consumer Credit Act, a credit
transaction would be (1) a sum of money that is made available by or on behalf of the first
party (the lender) to the second party (the borrower); and (2) the second party makes one
or more payments to the first party.
In bunq we do offer cards to our users which can be credit or debit. Both of them operate
in the same manner, in which the transaction is immediately debited from the account
(even in the credit card transactions). Even though there is no credit line, both types of
transactions are seen as credit by the merchants and Mastercard. We do this to support
our users to use their card in as many ways possible, so the user can also book a hotel or
rent a car, for example.
Originally this indicator was only applied to credit card companies (that is before the PSD
came into force) and the explanatory note of 1994 stated that is concerned "credit card
companies that issued credit cards that can be used for payments in shops, hotels, etc."
Since this indicator was originally targeted at credit card companies, this indicator should
be used when it is really a credit transaction. Therefore, for bunq, because there is no
credit facility and it is a credit card in name only but not by way of the product
characteristics, we don't report debit transactions with bunq cards above 15.000 euro.

8. Sanctions
Sanctions are political instruments in the foreign and security policy of the United Nations
(UN) and the European Union (EU). The UN Security Council can take measures to
maintain, or restore, international peace or security. Such measures range from economic
sanctions to international military action. Each UN member state is then called upon to
implement the requirements of a sanctions measure in its own territory. The EU applies
sanctions in pursuit of the specific objectives of the Common Foreign and Security Council
as set out in the Treaty of the European Union. EU sanctions are either adopted to ensure
compliance with UN sanctions requirements or enacted autonomously by the EU to
advance specific EU objectives. European sanctions regulations imposing sanctions apply
directly in EU member states.
Sanctions are mandatory instruments, used in response to breaches of international laws
and human rights, or to effect change when legal or democratic principles are not being
adhered to. Sanctions are used by international organizations, supranational bodies and
national governments in order to:
● achieve a change in the conduct of a particular country or regime;
● apply pressure on a particular country or regime to comply with set objectives;
● take certain types of enforcement action when international peace and security
may be threatened and diplomatic efforts have failed;
● seek to deter, prevent and suppress terrorist acts.
Sanctions measures imposed by the UN, EU or a country concern restrictive measures
against a country, regime, individual, service provider, industry or type of activity believed
to be violating international law and could include one or more of the following:
● the freezing of funds and other assets;
● the prohibition to provide financial services;
● a ban or restriction on trade;
● a ban or restriction on travel.

The ultimate objective of a sanction varies according to the situation. For instance, an
arms embargo and a ban on the export of certain items could be aimed at supporting a
peace process and restricting the financing of weapons by combatants. Sanctions may
also be aimed at preventing the proliferation of weapons of mass destruction, disrupting
terrorist operations, or trying to change the policies and actions of the target. Sanctions
are a tool used for enforcing foreign policy by putting pressure on a state or individual in
order to maintain or restore international peace and security. Often, sanctions are used as
an alternative to force. All recent UN and EU sanctions contain information as to their
intended aim or purpose.
Sanctions Regulations
The Sanctions Act 1977 (Sanctiewet 1977) is the legal basis in the Netherlands for the
implementation of international sanctions. National rules for the implementation of
obligations arising from treaties or binding decisions of international law organizations
end up in a ministerial regulation pursuant to the Sanctions Act 1977. The AFM and DNB
have been charged with the supervision of compliance of the Sanctions Act with respect
to financial institutions. To that end, the two supervisory authorities have jointly adopted
the Regulation on Supervision pursuant to the Sanctions Act 1977 (Regeling Toezicht
Sanctiewet 1977).
bunq ensures that it is always kept informed of the contents of the sanctions regulations

prohibitions set in sanctions regulations in a timely and adequate manner.

UK Sanctions
The United Kingdom imposes financial sanctions through its national framework,
governed by the Sanctions and Anti-Money Laundering Act 2018, subsequent to its
departure from the European Union. These sanctions are administered and enforced by
the Office of Financial Sanctions Implementation (OFSI). OFSI is responsible for providing
guidance on the interpretation of sanctions measures, maintaining the list of sanctioned
entities and individuals, and ensuring compliance with sanctions obligations. Firms and
individuals subject to UK jurisdiction must adhere to these sanctions, failing which OFSI
has the authority to impose monetary penalties or take legal action.

9. Off-boarding
We have no appetite to service users who are (suspected of) misusing our services longer
than legally required. Legally, even if a user is (suspected of) misusing their bunq account,
we have to follow our duty of care toward users for a period of time, as users have a right
to a bank account.
Taking into account our risk appetite and the law on the right to a (basic) bank account,
we have defined several non-exhaustive situations in which we believe to have a proper
reason to terminate a relationship with a user. These are described in our Off-boarding
Procedure.
If we have indications a user might be misusing our services, but the case is not strong
enough to warrant the termination of the relationship, the user will be: (i) investigated
further, (ii) reported to the FIU and/or (iii) be subject to further (enhanced) monitoring.
