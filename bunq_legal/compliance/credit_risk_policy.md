1. Introduction
At bunq, taking risks comes with the territory - it’s part of growing and doing bold things.
But we’re all about smart risks. That means we don’t gamble with stuff that doesn’t make
sense, and we always make sure our approach stays rock solid, sustainable, and in line
with what we stand for: Making Life Easy.
This policy is here to explain how we spot, assess, and handle credit risks - whether it’s
when we’re lending money or investing it. It also lays out how different bunq teams share
the responsibility to keep these risks in check. The goal? Making sure we only make smart
money moves, while always staying true to our values.
What is credit?
Credit is a way to borrow money or get access to a service now, and pay for it later. At
bunq, credit comes into play in two main ways:
Lending – We provide funds to borrowers for personal or business needs, and in return, we
receive interest on the loan. This interest is essentially a thank-you for allowing the
borrower to use our money for a while.
Investing – We buy securities to help grow our assets. Securities are financial instruments,
like stocks or bonds, that represent a share in a company or a debt investment. By
investing in these, bunq aims to increase its wealth over time.
These strategies help us manage our finances and continue to provide you with excellent
banking services.
What is credit risk?
Credit risk is the chance that someone (a bunq user, a business we lend to, or even
another financial institution) won’t be able to pay back what they owe us. If that happens,
bunq could lose money, which means we would have less available to support our own
business operations.
That's why it's crucial for us to carefully manage these risks, ensuring we only take chances
that align with our strategy, keeping a healthy balance between risk and reward.
Who does this policy apply to?
Short answer: everyone at bunq.

Purpose
At bunq, we know that smart growth comes with smart risks. This policy is our internal
guide to handling credit risks — making sure we stay financially strong so we can keep
giving Eva the best banking experience out there.
This policy provides guidance on:
Identifying credit risk (Section 5)
Assessing credit risk (Section 6)
Approving credit risk (Section 7)
Monitoring credit risk (Section 8)
Reporting credit risk (Section 9)
Measuring credit risk in relation to accounting and capital adequacy (Section 10)
Objectives
Credit risks are managed properly – We identify, assess, and handle risks in a structured
and consistent way.
Risk exposure stays within safe limits – We follow the guidelines in our Risk Management
Policy and Risk Appetite Statement to ensure we don’t take on more risk than we should.
Risks bring the right rewards – If we take on credit risk, we do so in a way that makes
financial sense - ensuring we get returns on our loans and investments.
What this policy doesn’t cover - It doesn’t go into the details of provisioning, impairment,
or capital adequacy assessments.

Scope
This policy applies to all of bunq’s and its subsidiaries' portfolios and investments that
involve credit risk, as outlined in our Risk Appetite Statement - which defines the level of
risk bunq is willing to take on in its operations.
We focus on three main types of credit risk:
Lending Risk – The risk we take when we lend money to someone and expect them to pay
us back.
Investment Risk – The risk that a company we invest in can’t pay what they owe.
Counterparty Risk – The risk that a financial partner (like a bank holding our non-euro
accounts) can’t hold up their end of the deal.
Each of these risks could potentially result in a financial loss for bunq. By following this
policy, we ensure that bunq grows safely and sustainably, delivering the best banking
experience possible.

4. Principles of Credit Risk Management
At bunq, managing credit risk isn’t just some checkbox exercise - it’s how we stay
financially strong so we can keep making life easy for Eva. We follow a Three Lines of
Defense model and stick to eight key principles that guide every decision we make when
it comes to credit risk.
The First line consists of the teams closest to our users - those who make decisions on
credit risks as part of bunq’s daily operations. They ensure that every accepted risk is
deliberate, aligned with our strategy, and monitored from start to finish.
The Second line is made up of the Compliance and Risk departments. Their role is to
oversee and support the First Line, making sure that all credit risks fit within bunq’s risk
appetite and regulatory framework. The Risk team acts as our Promise Keepers, ensuring
that we always deliver on our commitments to users.
The Third line is our independent Internal Audit team. They provide an unbiased
assessment of how well the First and Second Lines manage risk, ensuring that our
controls are solid and effective.
The Key Principles of Credit Risk Management
bunq applies 8 key credit principles to assess and manage credit risks.
1. Responsibility for the credit risk

2. Approvals
Every credit risk must go through a rigorous approval process before bunq takes it on. This
includes a deep analysis of the borrower, their financial stability, and how they plan to use
the credit. We ensure that the risk aligns with our user profile and that repayment is
realistic.
3. In-depth approach
We never evaluate credit risks in isolation. Every risk is assessed holistically, considering its
impact across multiple areas, including market conditions, liquidity, compliance, legal, and
operational risks.
4. Adequate reward
bunq only takes on credit risks when the expected return justifies the risk. If a potential
credit exposure does not provide an adequate reward, we do not accept it.
5. Diverse portfolio
To prevent excessive risk concentration, bunq ensures that its credit portfolio remains
diverse across industries, geographies, and counterparties. A key part of this strategy is the
One Obligor Exposure (OOE) concept. This concept helps us determine if multiple entities
should be seen as a single exposure in our risk assessments. The OOE concept is
important because it prevents us from placing too much risk on interconnected entities.
By recognizing groups of related entities as a single exposure, we can better manage
potential risks and ensure the safety of our investments. To identify when entities should
be considered connected, we use the following criteria:
● Control relationship – One entity has direct or indirect control over another.
● Economic dependency – If one entity were to face financial difficulties, it would
negatively impact the other.
In addition, other possible indicators of control should be taken into account, including:
● Decision-making power over the strategy or activities of another entity.

5. Identification of Credit Risk
To effectively manage credit risk, we first need to identify and understand it. At bunq, we
follow a structured process to catch potential risks upfront, so we’re never flying blind.
We identify credit risks by looking at these key components:
10

## Page 12

- Default risk – The chance that a borrower might fail to meet their repayment
obligations.
- Recovery risk – The risk that, in case of default, the borrower’s collateral (the stuff
they promised to secure the loan) isn’t sufficient to cover the outstanding debt.
- Migration risk – The possibility that a borrower’s credit rating changes, increasing
the risk of default, even if it hasn’t happened yet. Lower-rated borrowers require
bunq to set aside more capital to compensate for the increased risk.
- Spread risk – This is about reputation in the market. If a borrower’s credit quality
goes down (even if they’re still paying), the market value of their debt shrinks —
and that could make managing our exposure more expensive.
We also recognize that credit risk can be influenced by a range of external and internal
factors, which we refer to as risk drivers.
What drives credit risk?
bunq looks at multiple risk drivers to assess potential credit exposure:
● Obligor risk
○ The risk that a borrower (whether an individual, company, or financial
institution) doesn’t meet their obligations to bunq. This applies to anyone
with a financial relationship with bunq, including borrowers and guarantors.
When evaluating obligor risk, we consider factors such as:
■ Competitive landscape (for companies);
■ Management (for companies);
■ Current and projected financial standing (for companies and
individuals);
■ Collateral (for companies and individuals);
■ Legal structure of the deal (for companies and individuals);
■ Operational processes and their resilience (relevant for 3rd party
originators).

● Country risk
○ External factors that might prevent a borrower from repaying their credit
due to economic instability, currency exchange restrictions, or political
developments in their country.
● Credit concentration risk
○ A lack of diversification in the credit portfolio can increase exposure to a
specific borrower, industry, or region. We monitor:
■ Whether multiple borrowers are part of the same group.
■ If borrowers operate in the same geographic area or industry.
■ If economic events could impact several borrowers at once.
Credit risk isn’t just financial—it is also shaped by climate-related and environmental
factors: :
● Physical risk – The financial impact of climate change, including more frequent
extreme weather events, pollution, and biodiversity loss. These risks are particularly
pronounced in industries vulnerable to flooding, drought, and habitat destruction,
which can lead to higher default rates and depreciating collateral values.
● Transitional risk – The financial pressures businesses may face in adapting to
climate policies and sustainability regulations. Compliance costs, investments in
energy efficiency, and shifts in consumer preferences toward sustainable products
may reduce profitability and increase credit risk, particularly for high-carbon
industries.
All activities related to identifying credit risks should be registered in bunq’s Credit Risk
Identification and Assessment (CRIA) project in our internal systems. This structured
process ensures that risks are properly documented and evaluated as part of our ongoing
credit risk management.
6. Assessment of Credit Risk
At bunq, we don’t just say yes to credit risk and forget about it — we regularly check and
double-check every bit of credit exposure, whether it’s something new or something
we’ve already got in our portfolio. This way, we always know that the risks we take still fit

within our comfort zone. Every credit assessment happens through the Credit Risk
Identification and Assessment (CRIA) process. Here’s how it works:
● New proposals that exceed the thresholds set in the Risk Appetite Statement
undergo a CRIA review by the Treasury department before moving forward. This
ensures that risks are carefully considered before any financial commitment is

● For existing risk exposures, the First Line updates the CRIA at least once a year.

What happens if a risk level shoots up?
If a credit exposure shows a significant increase in risk, the Chief Risk Officer (CRO) may
place it on the Watch List—a category for risks that require more frequent monitoring and
reviews. Some credit risks, particularly those with higher exposure levels, may require
exceptions to the usual review cycle. In such cases, the CRO must approve the adjusted
assessment schedule as part of the Credit Authorization Matrix, a key internal procedure
ensuring proper governance over credit risks.
What about third-party originators?
Where credit origination or servicing is outsourced to an external party, the First Line
follows the bunq - Originator Due Diligence Guidelines to assess risk. For third-party
originators (including bunq subsidiaries), we evaluate not only credit risk but also
compliance and operational processes to ensure service continuity. Where available,
external ISAE 3402 or SOC II Type I and Type II reports may be relied upon for additional
verification.
Is the risk worth the reward?
Credit risk should only be accepted if the expected reward justifies the risk. As part of the
CRIA process, bunq analyzes the potential financial return on each credit exposure. The
Risk and Compliance departments then review the assessment and provide an opinion, as
outlined in the following section.

7. Approval of Credit Risk
No credit risk gets a free pass at bunq — every single one goes through a clear and
structured approval process to ensure that it aligns with our risk appetite, policies, and
regulatory obligations. Credit approvals can be for single investments or, when needed, for
entire portfolios, based on the relevant credit policy of the specific product.
Once a credit risk is approved, it must be monitored and managed by the relevant
employees, following bunq’s internal policies and external regulations.
The approval process works as follows:
● Step 1: Proposal by the First Line – The First Line submits a credit proposal
(investment, loan, or counterparty).
● Step 2: Risk Review by the Second Line – The Risk department conducts a Credit
Risk Identification and Assessment (CRIA) for any credit that exceeds the threshold
set in the Risk Appetite Statement. A competent Risk Officer must complete this
review.
● Step 3: Approval Based on Risk Level:
○ If the credit risk falls within the defined risk appetite, it is approved by the
Chief Risk Officer (CRO).
○ If the credit risk involves a new third-party originator, which is unrated, it
must be approved by the Management Board.
○ If the credit risk exceeds bunq’s predefined risk appetite, it requires
approval from the Supervisory Board.
Counterparty Limits Matter
Credit risks must always stay within the counterparty’s notional exposure limits—meaning
the maximum amount they can borrow must align with their own risk acceptance limits.
If a new transaction falls within an already approved counterparty limit, no additional
approval is required.

8. Monitoring
Once a credit facility is approved and documented, the monitoring phase begins. This
phase ensures that bunq maintains full visibility over credit risk exposures and can
respond quickly to any changes in risk.
At bunq, credit monitoring is essential to:
● Track the borrower’s financial health and usage of the facility.
● Identify early warning signs of risk, such as declining creditworthiness or falling
collateral values.
● Ensure compliance with loan conditions and financial agreements.
● Determine whether a borrower needs closer monitoring (e.g., added to the Watch
List) or if their loan should be classified as non-performing (for IFRS9 accounting).
Who is responsible for monitoring?
● The First Line is responsible for tracking credit risks and ensuring they stay within
approved limits.

● The Second Line (Risk & Compliance) oversees the monitoring tools used by the
First Line and performs independent checks. This includes periodic Risk and
Control Assessments (RCA) to ensure that monitoring practices remain effective.
How do we monitor credit risks?
bunq applies a structured Risk and Control Assessment (RCA) framework to regularly
track credit exposures and determine appropriate actions. As a minimum, monitoring
includes:
Quality checks – Continuous assessment of credit facilities, counterparties, and exposures,
ensuring they remain within approved limits (including currency fluctuations).
Default risk monitoring – Regular checks on the borrower’s default status, using bunq’s
internal definition of default.
Collateral value assessments – If collateral is required, we track its market value and
collectability over time.
Covenant compliance checks – Ensuring that the borrower follows all financial and
reporting agreements outlined in their contract.
Market risk tracking – Identifying economic or market fluctuations that could impact
credit risk (such as interest rate changes or sector-wide downturns).
Third-party originator monitoring – If a credit facility is outsourced, we conduct regular
credit, compliance, and operational checks on the originator. Where available, we rely on
external audit reports (ISAE 3402 or SOC II Type I and Type II) to confirm their processes
meet our standards.
By maintaining a rigorous, real-time monitoring process, bunq ensures that we remain
agile, responsive, and always in control of credit risks.

11. Roles and Responsibilities
Management Board (MB)
The Management Board delegates authority over individual credit exposures and
concentration limits to the Chief Risk Officer (CRO) and either the Chief Financial Officer

(CFO) or Chief Operating Officer (COO). However, for new third-party originators (whose

Capital Flow Group (CFG)
Since CFG (our subsidiary) manages its own credit risks, it follows its own approval process.
Any risks CFG takes on must fit within CFG’s own credit policies and Risk Appetite —

Compliance Committee (RCC). bunq’s CRO has a seat and blocking rights in this

First Line of Defense
The First Line of Defense (mainly the Treasury department) is responsible for processing
credit applications and ensuring that risks align with bunq’s risk appetite. Even when
bunq works with third-party originators, the First Line remains responsible for managing
credit risk according to bunq’s principles.
Chief Risk Officer (CRO)
The CRO reports on credit risk developments to the Risk & Audit Committee of the
Supervisory Board at least quarterly to ensure full transparency.
Risk Department
The Risk team oversees bunq’s credit risk management framework and is responsible for:
● Assessing individual credit risk requests, considering macroeconomic factors,
industry trends, and climate-related risks.
● Ensuring proper procedures are followed for approval and ongoing monitoring.
● Providing clear reporting on total credit risk exposure across bunq’s portfolio.
Throughout the lifecycle of any credit risk exposure, the First and Second Lines remain
responsible for monitoring, reporting, and controlling credit risk. Any negative
developments must be assessed and escalated to the appropriate decision-makers.
The CRO reports on credit risk developments to the Risk Committee of the Supervisory
Board at least quarterly to ensure full transparency.
