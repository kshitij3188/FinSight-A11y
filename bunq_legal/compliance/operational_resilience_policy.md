1. Introduction
Have you ever experienced any disruptions while using your favorite app? Pretty annoying,
isn’t it? In bunq our top priority is to ensure a continuity of our service on different levels,
because we know how critical bunq is for our users. This policy explains in detail how we
manage to maintain our service despite different risks, which may manifest during bunq’s
activity.
Two preliminary concepts are recalled here that allow to better contextualize this policy
into the overall Risk Management Framework and the general way of working at bunq.
2. Definitions
Ownerships
At bunq each employee (owner)is fully accountable for a defined area that usually
corresponds to a problem that needs to be addressed (ownership). This means that
Owners are also responsible for managing the risks of deviating from the desired
outcomes for their Ownership.
Eva’s Wish List
As with everything that bunq does, its archetype user – Eva – is at the center of bunq’s Risk
Management strategy. This means that bunq always focuses on the ultimate
consequence of suffering operational disruptions and losses: these events affect bunq’s
ability to serve Eva and satisfy her wishes. First line functions are assigned Ownerships
that are aligned with the delivery on Eva’s Wish List. The Second Line is the Promise
Keeper: essentially they supervise the 1st line’s ability to guarantee the delivery on Eva’s
Wish List using special business processes.
Operational Risk
In bunq 2nd line owners monitor performance and dig under the hood of the 1st line
ownerships in order to prevent disruptions and promote operational resilience.
bunq’s definition of operational risk is the risk of an operational disruption to the delivery
of the promises made to bunq users, due to ineffective processes, people, systems, or
external events.

2.1. Operational Resilience
bunq has defined Promise Keeper ownerships aimed at ensuring that effective controls
exist such that Operational Resilience can be guaranteed in the face of adversity and
potential disruptions within different operations. They are:
● Promise Keeper - Operational Continuity
● Promise Keeper - IT
● Promise Keeper - Third Parties
● Promise Keeper - Models
and within this policy they are referred to collectively as Operational Resilience Promise
Keepers.
Additionally, the Operational Resilience Promise Keepers shall analyze disruptions that
can’t be prevented and ensure bunq puts enough money aside to pay bills for restoration
costs.
When categorizing risks bunq uses the risk drivers that are presented in the Risk
Taxonomy from the Risk Management Policy and also looks at what we call the “primary
effect”, what happens first.
These risks are:
● regulatory sanctions (regulatory risks),
● financial losses (financial risks),
● operational disruptions (operational risks)
● or loss of user interest (reputational risks).
All of these are interconnected, with the ultimate effect always being a loss of trust in
bunq and eventually a loss of users. This “primary effect” categorization is used (and
useful) within the Risk Profile process and should not be confused with a criterion to
determine the scope of this policy: here we are also covering instances of operational risk
as a secondary effect of financial, reputational and regulatory risks.

6. Operational Risk profile and Appetite
bunq’s operational risk profile is assessed at the Risk Driver level within the Risk Profile
phase of the annual Eva’s Wish List update process cycle. The Risk Appetite is defined
thereafter.
This process ensures that the strategy and objectives of the Operational Resilience
Promise Keepers are aligned with the overall strategy and risk management objectives of
bunq as described in the Risk Appetite Statement approved by both the Managing Board
and Supervisory Board.
At an aggregate level, the Risk Appetite for Operational Risk is set by defining the types of
major operational risk events, for which bunq has no appetite. According to bunq’s 2024
Risk Appetite a risk event can be categorized as major based on one of three criteria:
● The (direct) financial loss resulting from it is higher than a set threshold
● The affected system is considered a critical functionality for Eva and the disruption
impacts more than a set number of users
● User data is breached/compromised and the breach impacts more than a set
number of users
It is the goal of the Operational Resilience Promise Keepers to limit major incidents by
applying the Risk Management Cycle to the 1st line activities (or Ownerships) that expose
bunq to such events. 1st line ownerships in scope of the Operational Resilience Promise
Keepers oversight may also have KPIs that are identified as suitable indicators of the
health of the ownership processes. In these cases such indicators are incorporated in the
Operational Risk KPIs framework, thus establishing a more detailed appetite for risk
applicable to the given ownership.

7.2 Inherent vs. Residual risk
We distinguish between “inherent risk” and “residual risk” of each ownership or risk
scenario within a process.
Inherent risk is assessed assuming there are no safeguards (“controls”) around the
ownership/process or any important safeguards failed.
Residual risk is assessed taking into consideration all established safeguards (controls) to
decrease the risk. If controls are not in place or non-effective the residual risk will be the
same as the inherent risks.
7.2.1 Risk Driver ranking
Within the annual Risk Profile exercise each risk driver in bunq’s taxonomy needs to be
assigned a residual risk ranking label.
bunq uses the following methodology to assess the materiality (= risk ranking) of the risk
drivers:
- HIGH (“H”): If the risk can:
- threaten the continuity of bunq by way of sanctions
- result in potential financial losses of > € 5 million
- cause operational disruption or an exodus of existing users / massive loss of
interest of potential users
- MEDIUM/HIGH (“M/H”): if the risk:
- can result in potential financial loss is between € 1 million and € 5 million
- threaten severe operational disruption materially affecting more than 20%
of users
- is a product/service feature that can lead to an outflow of more than 20% of
users / loss of more than 20% of expected user growth
- MEDIUM (“M”): if the risk:

7.4 Risk Response Selection
bunq treats identified and assessed operational risks applying one of the following four
techniques, also called risk response strategies: avoidance, mitigation, sharing and
transfer, acceptance.
Responses 1-3 are requested to 1st line owners by creating a Corrective Action Plan issue in
the dedicated project.
1. Risk avoidance: i.e. the strategy of “not taking the risk”. In a cost-benefit analysis
bunq opts for risk avoidance if the expected margin of activities is lower than the
expected risk cost taking into account all the risks. Such activities are abandoned
or not launched in the first place. Respective decisions have to consider several
aspects, such as time horizon, available specialized expertise, strategic objectives
and reputational risks. A decision to avoid risks in most cases is expected to be
taken at the stage of new product approval or upon detecting (persistent) negative
trends in collected internal loss data or KPI(s) related to a particular activity.
2. Risk mitigation: i.e. the strategy of “intelligently minimizing risks in their
development”. The objective may be a cause-oriented reduction of loss frequency
or an effect-oriented reduction of loss severity. The tools of risk mitigation mainly
include a multitude of organizational safeguards and control measures. In
particular, bunq mitigates operational risks by defining, documenting and
updating the relevant business processes (ownerships). Furthermore, bunq
mitigates operational risk by following strict rules for the assignment of duties and
responsibilities among and within the functions and a system of internal control
and supervision. The main principle for organizing work flows is to segregate the
business-generating functions from the recording and monitoring functions. An
important factor in operational risk mitigation is also the continuous development
and upgrading of strategic information and communication systems. Finally, the
process for setting KPI thresholds for each ownership serves as an additional risk
mitigation tool.

3. Risk sharing and transfer: i.e. the strategy of “intelligently passing on risks to third
parties”. The strategy is mainly of interest if a risk cannot or only inadequately is
reduced by internal controls or if the cost of controls is higher than the expected
loss. Another condition is that, in comparison with bunq’s risk appetite, the risk is
so high that it cannot simply be accepted. To share and/or transfer risks bunq uses
insurance and outsourcing of certain activities and functions.
4. Risk acceptance: i.e. the strategy of “deliberately taking certain risks in a targeted
way”. As a rule, risk acceptance depends on a cost-benefit analysis or weighting the
expected income versus risk. A rational reason for accepting risks would be that
the expected loss is lower than the cost of management activities to mitigate the
risks. A decision to accept risks can be taken at the stage of new product approval
or upon analyzing particular loss events or risk KPI developments, or as a result of a
Risk and Control Assessment. Such decisions are prepared systematically and
documented:

b. The scenario is categorized using impact and probability scores as per the

8. Control Categorization
Controls are (sub-) processes that are designed and implemented (to be safeguards and)
to maintain the variability of outcomes of a given process within an acceptable range. The
variability of outcomes of a 1st line ownership corresponds to a variability of Eva’s
satisfaction with bunq’s services.
Controls are how inherent risks connected to an ownership can be reduced to acceptable
residual risks.

8.1 Control types
We adopt the following definitions of control types:
● Preventive: controls that are designed to stop errors and anomalies from
happening altogether;
● Corrective: controls that are designed to correct the errors and anomalies once
they are discovered;
● Directive: controls that are defined as formal procedures to be followed, which, if
adhered to, lead to the desired outcome without deviations;
● Detective: controls aimed at the timely detection of deviations once they have
happened.
bunq’s strategy and agile way of working as a startup has influenced the approach
towards control design by creating a preference for corrective and detective measures.
The ability to detect and correct quickly deviations is critical to minimize impact while
maintaining the underlying processes lean and allowing them to change frequently.
The current risk strategy aims at creating a healthy mix of different control types around
each 1st line ownership.
8.2 Control effectiveness
Within the Risk and Control Assessment process the effectiveness of existing controls is
assessed. Effectiveness is evaluated at three different levels:
● Design: Evaluation of whether a control, individually or in combination with other
implemented controls, is capable of effectively preventing or detecting and
correcting errors;
● Operating effectiveness: assessment of whether the control as implemented
operates in line with its design;
● Sustainability: assessment of whether the control can remain effective over time
and with changing circumstances.
8.3 Control effectiveness rating
Controls are assigned an effectiveness rating that can be one of the following:
21

## Page 23

● Effective: the control can consistently achieve its desired results;
● Non effective: the control cannot consistently achieve the desired risk mitigation
● Partly effective: these controls are those that successfully mitigate some (most) of
the risk, but for which deviations are noted
The rating is assigned separately to control (ii) design and (ii) to its operating effectiveness.
Controls are also assigned a sustainability rating that is either:
● Sustainable, or
● Not sustainable.

9.4 Risk Key Performance Indicators
Monitoring of risk drivers in the scope of the Operational Resilience Promise Keepers is
done on a continuous basis using well defined KPIs. This allows us to determine whether
the outcome of the analyzed processes is deviating from the desired operating
parameters.
The selection of KPIs to be monitored happens in multiple ways:
● Within an Ownership review. Each Ownership is documented as described in the
Ownership risk control process. Ownerships, therefore, have KPIs. Operational
Resilience Promise Keepers select among these indicators that highlight the
downside risk of the given ownership.
● Within the root cause analysis process. In case an action defined by a 5-why
analysis represents the creation of a KPI, the Promise Keeper may select such KPI
to be included in the framework.
● As a corrective action (CAP item) resulting from a Risk and Control Assessment or
an audit.
In all cases the KPI must be added by the process owner to the ownership document.
Risk KPIs are categorized as

● Risk Appetite KPIs, in case they are selected to represent the overall health status
of a Risk Driver in Eva’s Wish List. This happens during the annual Risk Appetite
updating process.
● Operational KPIs, in case they are defined to monitor a process with a higher
granularity.
Each indicator has 2 thresholds (Level 1 and 2). For Operational KPIs a breach of the Level 1
threshold is escalated to the respective Department Head and of Level 2 - to the Promise
Keeper. For Risk Appetite KPIs a breach of the Level 1 threshold is escalated to the Promise
Keeper; a breach of the Level 2 threshold is reported to MB/SB in the monthly
management report. Sometimes there will be a Level 3 threshold - a regulatory limit, if it
exists. It is expected that Level 1 and Level 2 thresholds for internal risk management
purposes are set somewhat below hard regulatory requirements. For business KPIs (but
not for risk KPIs) in most cases we will also have a Level 0 threshold representing an
ambitious business goal (to be used for employee achievement recognition purposes), but
that threshold is outside of the scope of the Operational Resilience Policy and will not be
considered in this document.
All KPI breaches are reported to the MB and SB within the monthly reporting pack. Since
KPIs may have different monitoring periods, breaches of KPIs with higher monitoring
frequency are notified between monthly reporting cycles using a dedicated Gitlab project:
KPI-monitoring. This ensures that the Promise Keeper is alerted in a timely manner of
breaches and that these are escalated to the CRO within the weekly reporting cycle.
KPI breaches are closed when a new measurement of the indicator results in a value no
longer beyond the relevant level threshold.
At every breach event the corrective actions presented by the process owners and their
timelines shall be ack-ed at the respective level. This ensures the Risk Management
function has a full picture of the operational resilience status of internal processes at all
times.
9.5 Root cause analysis
Major incidents (and near-misses) require the execution of a root cause analysis. This is a
requirement since the inception of the company because of its ambition to always be a
learning organization. The analytical tool chosen for this activity is the 5 Whys analysis.
27

## Page 29

A 5 why analysis is an iterative interrogative technique used to explore the
cause-and-effect relationships underlying a particular problem. It starts with asking first
the reason a given event occurred and then goes deeper asking the same question about
the response, creating a chain of reasons until the root cause is identified.
All requests for such an analysis go through a single process and are logged in a
centralized 5-why project in Gitlab. The Promise Keeper reviews all 5 Why’s related to
incidents categorized as Major, and has the authority to challenge actions that are
deemed insufficient to prevent the event from occurring again. Results of reviews by
Promise Keepers are submitted to the CRO.
The Operational Resilience Promise Keepers also have the duty to challenge actions that
are deemed insufficient to achieve a good level of protection from a repeated occurrence
of the given incident. Key mitigation actions defined in a root cause analysis may be
promoted by the relevant Promise Keeper to be tracked in the Corrective Actions Plan
project. Not all actions are considered “key”, as some might address a specificity of the
analyzed event rather than a more generic scenario that requires the implementation of a
corresponding control.
Within the root cause analysis the Promise Keeper also nudges the process owners to
create KPIs for the analyzed ownership, with the purpose of monitoring deviations of the
process from the desired performance parameters.
9.6 Corrective Actions Plan
The Corrective Actions Plan (CAP) project is part of the Promise Keeper Expectation

external audit, a root cause analysis.
Corrective Actions created within the project must be categorized using labels that allow
aggregating data from the project for reporting purposes: the basic categorization
includes an Eva Wish List risk driver label, a Corrective actions categorization label, a label

compliance assessment, internal or external audit) and finally a label identifying the team
of the action owner.

Each Corrective Action must also have a due date. This deadline is negotiated between

12. Pillar 2 Capital for Operational Risk
The methodology to quantify required capital for operational risk is based on the
definition, analysis and combination of a set of scenarios.
Based on historical data regarding incidents and near misses, as well as risks already
flagged in the Risk Register, or within bunq’s Risk Profile exercise, a list of realistic
scenarios is defined. Up to 5 of the most relevant scenarios are selected by the Head of
Risk and approved by the Chief Risk Officer to be used for an in-depth assessment that
leads to a more accurate quantification of the associated probability and impact for each
scenario.
At this point the occurrence of the 5 scenarios is combined, from none to all at once. A
probability threshold (99.9%) is defined for the cumulative probability and a final
combined scenario selected such that it’s the first whose probability is above the
pre-defined threshold. The loss associated with this scenario is the value to be used for
Operational Risk stress testing and Pillar 2 capital.
The full details of this approach are described in a dedicated document, the Operational
Risk Scenarios Methodology.
