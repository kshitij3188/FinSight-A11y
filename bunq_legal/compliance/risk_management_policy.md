1. Introduction
As a fintech and neobank, bunq faces risks that are common within the financial industry
and that are necessary for growth. In order to make life easy for our user, Eva, we need to
take these risks because they come with the benefits of a better service and a better tech
and financial product for Eva. To make sure that the risk we take on is sustainable, we have
a detailed and far-reaching risk management system within bunq, which is constantly
monitored and updated.
This policy will outline the approach bunq takes to risk, the cycle that we practice as we
become aware of potential risk and the steps we take to tackle it, the governance
structure in bunq that manages risk, and how we update our list of risks.
2. Purpose and Scope
The purpose of this policy is to define how bunq approaches risk and illustrate the risk
management framework it uses. bunq follows the requirements set by the EU Directive
2013/36/EU “on access to the activity of credit institutions and the prudential supervision
of credit institutions and investment firms” as outlined by the European Banking
Authority (EBA) Guidelines on Internal Governance (EBA-GL-2017-11).
This policy applies to all bunq departments and staff, including that of branches.
Subsidiaries of bunq can have their own risk management systems and, in that sense, are
outside the scope of this policy. However, the subsidiaries are under risk oversight of bunq,
meaning bunq monitors the approaches of its subsidiaries to risk.
This policy is leading over policies for specific risks, but is preceded by bunq’s Articles of
Association, the Rules of the Managing Board, the Rules of the Supervisory Board
(including its sub-committees), and bunq’s Decision Structure document.
3. Risk Management Approach
Every company is different in how it approaches, manages, and mitigates risk. bunq’s
philosophy on dealing with risk is woven into how the company and its functions are
structured. This section will introduce bunq’s approach to risk management.
1. User-centric company
The center of bunq’s universe is the user. To guide our activities and goals according to
the needs of the user, bunq created an archetype user, Eva. Serving Eva’s needs so she can

enjoy the benefits of having a bunq account to the fullest is one of the main objectives of
bunq. Risk management activities have therefore been organized in line with this
objective. This makes our risk management consistent and thorough.
2. Approach to risk
bunq adopts the Three Lines of Defense Model to manage risks.
1. The First line of defense is user-facing, which means that this line of
defense is in direct connection with the users and the company’s business
objectives. The objective of the First line is to serve the wishes and needs of
Eva.
2. The Second line of defense consists of Compliance and Risk functions, led
by the Head of Compliance and the Head of Risk. The objective of the
Second line is to monitor and support the first line in keeping the promises
made to the user. Second Line employees are therefore referred to as
Promise Keepers.
3. The Third line of defense is the objective and independent checker of the
design and internal controls that are aimed at satisfying Eva’s Wish List. The
risks that the First and the Second lines face make up the Third line’s Audit
Universe.
This model will be described in more detail in Section 5.
3. Risk culture
bunq has a number of principles and mechanisms in place that promote a healthy risk
culture. Each employee is trained in the following core principles, to promote such culture:
- Each employee has their Ownerships. Ownership is the word for “responsibility”
used in bunq. An Ownership is created when collected data shows that there is a
problem (for Eva or Adam, which is bunq’s archetype of an employee). Then, the
problem gets assigned to an employee as their own responsibility, their Ownership.
They will take charge of this Ownership and collect data to evaluate the progress
toward solving the underlying problem.
The Ownerships are distributed between the lines of defense. The First line of defense are
in charge of their Ownerships, with the Second line reviewing their ownerships and, if
3

## Page 5

needed, challenging the risk ranking. The Second and Third lines also perform risk
assessments and control testing with a frequency that depends on the severity of the risk
connected to the Ownership. For instance, procedures of the First line ownerships with
the highest (inherent) risks are reviewed at least annually (and approved) by the Second
line to ensure the effectiveness of control design.
- Decisions are data-centric, meaning decisions are made based on data/evidence of
a problem. From a risk perspective, this means assessing risks based on the
available data and the level of risk they represent. This approach manifests in all
Ownerships having Key Performance Indicators (KPIs), which are specific tasks that
the employee in charge of an Ownership record as indicators of progress toward
solving the issue that their Ownership represents.
- Effective communication is an important feature of bunq-style working. Every
Adam upon beginning their journey with bunq learns how to avoid
miscommunication and how to follow the guidelines for effective communication.
bunq’s Risk and Compliance Officers reinforce these principles by:
- Quality-checking the definitions of Ownership in our designated register. The
definitions include the parameters that place the Ownerships within the Risk
Taxonomy of bunq and assess their inherent risks and risks that remain after the
issue is treated.
- Instructing the First line to adopt KPIs that track the status of their Ownership and
identify issues that may affect Eva or bunq.
Two more processes contribute to bunq’s healthy risk management culture:
- Incident management. Every employee is encouraged to speak up when they have
a concern about a (potential) issue. When an issue is brought up, a company-wide
process is kicked off to determine whether an issue is actually occurring. All
incidents are registered and categorized so that bunq can learn from them and
prevent them from happening again. These records influence the planning of risk
assessments and audits, as well as reduce the time needed to identify any
(potential) issues in the future.
- “5 Whys”. If an issue occurred this instrument for analysis is used to determine the
root causes of the incident and why the incident occurred. All employees are

trained to perform such an analysis when an issue occurred, including for their
own natural mistakes without being blamed. Every obstacle and issue that arises is
seen as an opportunity to learn. After the 5 Whys analysis is complete, the result is

Depending on the seriousness of the incident, Risk Management may monitor the
corrective actions determined by the 5 Whys analysis.
4. An emphasis on reactive measures
Having grown from a start-up to a scale-up, bunq is a fast-growing company, where risks
are taken with the understanding that changes cause risk and that such risks can be
detected by real-time monitoring and be instantly prevented/mitigated. This approach is
part of the IT strategy of the company. At the same time, bunq is always looking to
improve and strengthen its risk management, introduce more preventative measures,
and have frequent reviews of business processes that bear risks.
5. Eva’s Wish List
Eva’s Wish List is a risk classification table used in bunq to:
- Ensure that everything we do is in line with Eva’s wishes;
- Enable a complete and effective risk identification and assessment;
- Clarify Ownership scopes in the first and second line;
- Facilitate risk-related data reporting and collection.
Eva’s Wish List has three levels:
1. High-level Wishes;
2. Granular (specific) wishes;
3. Risk Drivers (or simply Risks).
The Risk Taxonomy is built by taking the wishes of bunq’s users and connecting each wish
with a more specific and detailed wish, and connecting those with related risk drivers. An
example would be Eva’s desire for efficiency (wish) connected to a specific wish to have a
fast onboarding (specific wish), which can be associated with onboarding experience risks.
Further splits into smaller sub-categories happen at the level of relevant policies. For
example, climate risks are recognized, assessed, and managed by bunq, but are not a

separate category in the Eva’s Wish List. They have relevant and specific policies
addressing them instead. The full taxonomy is reviewed at least annually.
Risk aggregation is done using the structure of the Eva’s Wish List. This taxonomy, in turn,
can be mapped to “traditional” definitions of risk, such as, for example, “operational risk” as
defined by the Principles for the Sound Management of Operational Risk, Basel II.
The Risk Taxonomy is also adopted within bunq as Audit Universe, which is a collection of
potential audit activities that are performed by the Third Line of Defense, the System
Checker (Internal Audit) Department.
4. Risk Management Cycle
bunq’s Risk Management and Compliance work is articulated into Ownerships and those
Ownerships include different tasks and responsibilities meant to monitor how risks listed
in Eva’s Wish List are managed. A similar approach is applied for the different phases of
the Risk Management Process (or Cycle), which is the process of assessment and
treatment of risks. It involves multiple steps, which are covered by Risk Management
Ownerships and involve backward and forward-looking approaches:
1. Risk identification
The First line is responsible for the identification (and management) of risks. The Second
line, in turn, must ensure that risks are identified correctly. For that, the Second line
maintains the following ownerships:
- The Product Approval and Review Process (PARP): a future-oriented evaluation of
product proposals or significant internal process changes, within which risks to
both Eva and bunq are identified and assessed.
- For example, when a proposal is made for a new feature in the bunq app
we assess scenarios where we might receive a high volume of inquiries
about the feature. We ensure that our customer support team is
well-equipped with sufficient information and resources to handle all
queries. Additionally, we verify that the proposal aligns with relevant
regulatory requirements and scrutinize other facets of the proposal.
- Risk and Control Assessments: an annual plan of analysis of the First line
Ownerships to identify and quantify inherent and residual risk exposures and
6

## Page 8

assess the effectiveness of implemented measures to mitigate the risk (control
testing).
- These assessments are performed by the Second line to ensure that bunq’s
processes work well, that the design of such processes is clear, and to check
how such processes work in practice. These checks are performed in light of
risk that may arise. An example is evaluating the user data deletion process
for regulatory compliance. Initially, a risk is identified concerning the
potential failure to process data within the regulation-mandated 30-day
period. Next, the process design, specifically the relevant procedure, is
reviewed to confirm that it mandates the deletion of user data within 30
days. Finally, sample data deletion requests are examined to ensure they
were indeed processed within the stipulated 30-day timeframe.
- The annual Risk Profile exercise: a backward and forward-looking exercise,
discussed in Section 6.
- This exercise is an annual review and update of bunq’s Risk Profile, allowing
us to be prudent with how we approach risk, how much risk we can accept,
and how much attention we give to different risks (the higher the risk, the
more attention).
- A Third Party Risk Management framework: backward and forward-looking process
with a specific focus on suppliers and other third parties integrated in bunq’s
systems.
- This framework allows us to identify and manage risks related to partnering
with third parties. For more information please see our Third Party Risk
Management Policy.
- The SIRA (Systematic Integrity Risk Analysis): document and procedure which
represents the continuous identification and assessment of integrity risks that
bunq faces when it offers its products to the public
- An example of a risk that SIRA identifies and assesses would be money
laundering.

ownership, in which case they include Risk Identification, or smaller assessments
which focus on quantifying the risk to a specific area within an Ownership.
Assessments can be ad-hoc or recurring. The recurring ones include
- The review Treasury and Finance controls
- The review of Credit origination and monitoring activities by the first line, by
subsidiaries and other originators
- Ongoing monitoring of the IT Control Framework

- Internal Capital Adequacy Assessment Process (ICAAP) and Internal Liquidity

against the risk appetite (the threshold of how much risk the company can
accept), with a focus on:
- Adequacy of regulatory capital and liquidity buffers
- bunq must hold an adequate amount of capital and liquidity based
on regulation. If we do not possess such an amount, we cannot

- Analysis of operational risk scenarios
- Analyzing risk scenarios that may affect bunq’s operations allows us
to be financially ready for situations when real risks must be
addressed. For example, when the Covid-19 pandemic had a big
impact on the health of a great number of people across the world.
If a similar health-related issue arises, bunq’s workforce, Adams, may

backup force will be needed to cover their shifts that would mean
additional costs that need to be added to capital requirements in
advance. Analysis of such a scenario allows us to be ready for such
situations and to have funds to hire people that can cover the tasks
of every absent worker.
- Determining concentrations of risk on bunq’s balance sheet
- Balance sheet concentration may lead to unsustainable credit risk
exposures. For example, if bunq provides credit only to businesses
that manufacture in a specific industry. If the industry goes through
a crisis, or even disappears, bunq will suffer losses on a big scale, as
there will be no credits left that are profitable.
- Stress testing the capacity of risk that can be borne by bunq’s capital and
liquidity ratios
- Testing how much stress can bunq’s prudential buffers take without
causing harm to bunq’s business operations and commitments. An
example of such a test would be to determine what would be the
impact on bunq if 20% of its users ended their relationship with
bunq. The test determined whether bunq capital and liquidity ratios
would be able to absorb such a loss and meet the liquidity and
capital needs of regulation and the remaining users.

3. Risk monitoring and mitigation
For identified risks, bunq sets limits within which it is willing to accept them and monitors
the exposure to these risks against the limits. This is referred to as the Risk Appetite
operationalization phase which follows the Risk Appetite Statement.
A risk assessment may lead to the conclusion that bunq is taking a higher risk than what
is defined as acceptable under its risk appetite or that such risk can be reduced. In this
case, Risk Management and Compliance start to monitor the corrective actions
implemented by the First line. For example, if something on our website is not described
clearly enough for all users to understand, a corrective action plan will be proposed,
adopted, and acted on by the First line and the Second line will ensure everything is done
efficiently and with high quality. The tools for Risk Monitoring and Mitigation are:
- The Risk Appetite KPI Monitoring project. A centralized register to note deviations
from the nominal operating parameters and track resolutions of such deviations.
By setting early warning thresholds this tool allows forward-looking course
corrections. On top of strategic KPIs defined in the Risk Appetite Statement, the
project also monitors operational KPIs of 1st line ownerships that are set for more
specific risks. This ensures additional early warnings before the strategic Risk
Appetite KPIs are breached. Further, the project monitors the Risk Appetite KPIs of
branches and subsidiaries, whenever relevant.
- The Corrective Actions Plan is a unified register of actions that were identified as a
suitable treatment of risks that must be reduced (or could benefit from a
reduction).
- Incidents and 5-Whys monitoring: the Second line reserves the right to challenge
the effectiveness of solutions identified by the First line to issues analyzed within
the 5-Whys analyses, especially those assigned in the wake of risk incidents.
- Transaction filtering, monitoring, and user/counterparty screening: to ensure
integrity risks are under control.
- Counterparty/investment (news) monitoring: to ensure credit risks are under
control.
- Central overview & calendar of Policies and a centralized register of charters:
monitoring of policies by Risk Management, also referred to as the “Policy House”.

5. Governance
This section describes the Governance structure within bunq that facilitates Risk
Management.
The Supervisory Board and the Management Board structure the governance of bunq in
order to achieve its business objectives. They do so while creating a steady and efficient

6. Eva’s Wishlist Update Process
1. Risk Profile
The Risk Profile is a document containing the identifications and assessments of the
inherent and residual risk that bunq is exposed to by engaging in the activities typical of a
bank. The risk drivers that underlie Eva’s wishes are given risk ratings, such as Low,
Medium, Medium/High, and High.
When the Risk profile is updated, Eva’s Wish List is also updated. The updates occur as
part of the annual update or if deemed necessary. The Risk Profile is a Second Line
Ownership and its principles and procedures are documented in separate documents.
2. Risk Appetite Statement
The Risk Appetite Statement of bunq is a document that defines the risk appetite, i.e. the
amount of reasonably foreseeable risk that Eva and bunq – given its business objectives
and operating environment – are willing to accept to pursue bunq’s strategy and business
plan.
More specifically the purpose of the Risk Appetite Statement is to
a. Outline Eva’s and bunq’s risk appetite in relation to its strategy
b. Define the amount of risk Eva and bunq are willing to take for each risk in
Eva’s Wish List

bunq expresses its risk appetite for each element of Eva’s Wish List using qualitative
appetite statements supported, where needed, by quantitative KPIs with thresholds.
When assigning a level of appetite for a risk, bunq takes into account the maximum
amount of risk it can technically afford to take before breaching one or more of its capital
base, liquidity, borrowing capacity, or (other) regulatory constraints and given its current
level of resources. This means that internal limits are always set within the legal and
regulatory requirements.
Risk Appetite setting also takes into account risk exposures of subsidiaries and branches,
and can be considered as the “Group Risk Appetite”. Subsidiaries and branches can
establish their own risk appetites for more granular/specific risk monitoring which are set,
approved and monitored by their Management Boards within the involvement of the
Supervisory Board of bunq (unless specifically requested).
The Risk Appetite is a Second line ownership and its principles and procedures are
documented with further details in a separate document.
3. Annual Cycle
The Risk Appetite annual update cycle is as follows:
1. The Risk Management consults all relevant department heads and updates the
organization’s Risk Profile document, including the Risk Taxonomy (Eva’s Wish
List). Each risk driver in Eva’s Wish List is a risk ranking based on the potential
impact.
2. A Risk Plan is defined, with actions to (re)assess, mitigate, or control the risks in
Eva’s Wish List. The Plan is defined based on the risk ratings and updated within
the year when deemed necessary. We note that regular risk assessments to be
performed by the 2nd line during the year are part of the Annual Plan and are
described in the Risk and Control Assessment ownership.
3. Based on the updated Risk Profile, the Managing Board reviews the Risk Appetite
Statement at least annually and updates it in case changes are needed. Within the
Risk Appetite, a set of indicators is defined to be used as key indicators, each with
certain tolerance levels, and to be reported monthly to the Managing Board and
Supervisory Board.
