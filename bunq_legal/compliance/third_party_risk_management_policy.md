Summary
This policy describes bunq’s approach to dealing with risks that arise from partnering with
third parties for outsourcing and non-outsourcing purposes. Sections 1, 2 3 and 4 set the
stage by presenting repent definitions, specifics, objectives and roles related to Third Party
Risk Management. Section 5 outlines the steps that are taken for a Third Party to create a
relationship with bunq that involves performing services for us or on our behalf. Section 6
lists the responsible people to whom disagreements can be brought in relation to the
processes described in this policy. Section 7 concludes this policy with additional remarks
concerning Conflicts of Interest and Data Protection.

1. Introduction
In the financial sector, taking risks is a natural part of life that cannot be avoided. Every
company in the sector must therefore maintain and follow a healthy and sustainable risk
policy. bunq actively applies a prudent and risk-aware approach to all of its operations,
including its relationships with Third Parties.
Third Parties who are engaged by bunq in order to support the execution of its business
activities are defined as “suppliers”. They can form a critical component of bunq’s
operations. The sudden collapse or continued underperformance of such suppliers can
undermine our ability to serve our user, Eva.

2. Purpose and Objectives
To avoid any situations where negative effects from engaging with a Third Party affect our
ability to serve Eva and to ensure continuity of our service, bunq has put a number of
safeguards against Third Party Risk within its structure and operations to guarantee
bunq’s ability to serve Eva can withstand any issues that arise on the side of our suppliers.
These measures form a process for assessing third parties before and during bunq’s
relationship with them. This approach makes up the core of this policy and is comprised of
4 steps:
1. Selection of Third Parties
2. Onboarding of Third Parties
3. Monitoring of Third Parties
4. Offboarding of Third Parties
This process guides employees of bunq (who are known as Adam) to make risk-informed
decisions and manage the third party risk to an acceptable level. In return, it ensures a
high level of service continuity to Eva.
bunq’s approach to Third Parties, including outsourcing and ICT Third Party service
supplier is based on the following guidelines:
● Bank of England/PRA Supervisory Statement SS2/21, Outsourcing and third party
risk management;
● Decree on Prudential Rules for Financial Undertakings (Besluit prudentiele regels –
Bpr);
● Digital Operational Resilience Act;
● DNB (the Dutch Central Bank) recommendations on outsourcing;
● EBA guidelines on outsourcing arrangements;
● EBA recommendations on outsourcing to cloud service providers;
● Finanƒl Supervision Act (Wet op het financieel toezicht – Wft);
● Guidelines on the use of Remote Customer Onboarding Solutions; and

3. Scope
This policy applies to all employees of bunq and its branches, but excludes subsidiaries.
bunq’s subsidiaries manage their third party relationships according to their own policy.
However, oversight of subsidiaries to ensure their supplier management practices are
proper is within the scope of bunq’s Third Party Risk Management policy.
3.1 Repetitive, critical to core functions and ICT services
This policy includes all Third Parties providing services of repetitive nature that are critical
to core functions of bunq. Third Parties providing recurring ICT services are within the
scope of this policy regardless of the criticality of the services to bunq. A core function is
anything that significantly affects the way bunq provides financial services to Eva,
including Eva onboarding and compliance management, and two-way communication
with Eva and the supporting processes. These conditions are set to exclude typical office
supplies, isolated and one-off decisions of no critical nature to meet Eva’s expectations,
specifically because they are non-repetitive (thus posing limited operational and financial
risks). It should be noted that Financial Crime risks associated with this kind of suppliers
are mitigated by the Finance Department using the invoice execution process and
Compliance Operations using transaction filtering.
3.2 Who exactly is a “supplier”?
For the purpose of this policy, the definition of “suppliers” excludes internal staff, clients
and/or deposit holders and correspondent banks or other regulated payment service
providers such as non-critical Payment Service Providers and Electronic Money
Institutions providing correspondent and other financial services (that do not fall under
the definition of outsourcing). Oversight of these partner financial institutions is covered
by the Promise Keeper. Nonetheless, Payment Service Providers that impact more than
20% of Personal and/or Business Eva, including an outflow of current Eva or loss of
expected user growth, fall under the definition of supplier. Any exceptions must obtain
approval from the Risk department via Third Parties Risk Manager. External staff is also
excluded from the scope of this policy, as such relationships are addressed by HR policies.
However, agencies that provide external staff and/or support (such as payroll agencies or
consulting firms) are considered “suppliers” and fall in the scope of this policy.

3.3 Outsourcing
This subsection will specify what falls under the definition of outsourcing, what conditions
must be met for an outsourcing, what is considered material, cloud, or sub-outsourcing,
and what is considered a non-outsourcing supplier.
A supplier falls under the definition of an outsourcing service provider if it provides
services or activities which qualify as outsourcing within the meaning of the EBA
guidelines:
1. It performs a function (or part thereof) that falls within the scope of functions that
would or could realistically be performed by bunq, even if bunq has not performed
this function in the past itself; and
2. The function (or a part thereof) is performed by the supplier on a recurrent or an
ongoing basis.
For such Third Parties, the Third Party owner must complete the risk assessment using
the DNB outsourcing risk analysis template, including a CIA rating.

3.3.2 Material outsourcing
In relation to OSPs, the Third Party owner needs to determine whether the outsourced
service constitutes a material outsourcing. To such outsourcing arrangements, a stricter
risk governance is applied.
If any of the below questions are answered positively, the outsourced function should be
classified as critical or important, hence, a material outsourcing:
● Would a defect or failure in the performance of the outsourced functions
materially impair:
○ bunq’s continuing compliance with the conditions of bunq’s authorization
or bunq’s other obligations under Directive 2013/36/EU (CRD), Regulation
(EU) No 575/2013 (CRR), Directive 2014/65/EU (MIFID), Directive (EU)
2015/2366 (PSD2) and Directive 2009/110/EC (E-money) and bunq’s
regulatory obligations? Or
○ bunq’s financial performance? Or
○ The soundness or continuity of bunq’s banking and payment services and
activities?
● If operational tasks of internal control functions are outsourced, would a failure to
provide the outsourced function or the inappropriate provision of the outsourced
function have an adverse impact on the effectiveness of the internal control
function?
● Would the provision of the outsourced function require an authorization by a
competent authority?
In case of material outsourcing, the Third Party owner must notify the Third Parties Risk
Manager to inform DNB and the relevant national competent authorities supervising
non-EEA branches.
3.3.3 Cloud outsourcing
Cloud outsourcing refers to outsourcing of services delivered using cloud technology.
Cloud computing is a model for enabling ubiquitous, convenient, on-demand network
access to a shared pool of configurable computing resources (e.g. networks, servers,

storage, applications and services) that can be rapidly provisioned and released with
minimal management effort or service provider interaction.
In case of outsourcing to a cloud service provider, the EBA Recommendation On
Outsourcing To Cloud Service Providers (EBA/REC/2017/03) applies. bunq should inform
DNB of material cloud outsourcing with the required information.
3.3.4 Sub-outsourcing
Sub-outsourcing occurs if one or more suppliers, other than the principal OSP, but
commissioned by the principal OSP, perform elements of the outsourced activities. The
OSP is allowed to sub-outsource activities, provided that the sub-delegate complies with
the same outsourcing arrangements as we concluded with the OSP.
The Third Party owner is required to determine all instances of sub-outsourcing. If a
subcontractor in turn outsources activities to another Third Party, an outsourcing chain is
created.
3.3.5 Non-eligible outsourcing
The following cannot be outsourced.
1. Core management functions:
a. setting the risk strategy, risk management policy and risk-bearing capacity
of bunq;
b. setting the internal control framework and ongoing oversight and control
of the operation of bunq’s processes; and
c. the related responsibility to customers and the supervisory authority.
2. The outsourcing of functions cannot result in the delegation of the management
body’s responsibilities;
3. Outsourcing arrangements should not hinder effective (on-site or off-site)
supervision of bunq, and should not contravene any supervisory restrictions on
services and activities.
The Third Party owner should also consider the following:

considered as outsourcing in accordance to the EBA guidelines:

statutory audit;
2. Market information services (e.g. provision of data by Bloomberg, Moody’s,
Standard & Poor’s, Fitch);
3. Global network infrastructures (e.g. Visa, MasterCard);
4. Clearing and settlement arrangements between clearing houses, central
counterparties and settlement institutions and their members;
5. Global financial messaging infrastructures that are subject to oversight by relevant
authorities;
6. Correspondent banking services; and
7. The acquisition of services that would otherwise not be undertaken by bunq. Refer
to Annex VI for a list of examples.

5. Third-Party Risk Management Approach and Measures
As was stated in Section 2, the approach to Third Party Risk Management is structured
along 4 stages: selection, onboarding, monitoring, and offboarding. This section will
describe each stage in detail. Some sections will include gray boxes that will include
details that are specific to outsourcing service providers, which are Third Parties to who
bunq outsources some of its functions.
Stage 1: Selection
In order to guarantee the best possible product, bunq needs to choose the best possible
suppliers. For this purpose, the selection stage has been developed. The goal of this step is
to identify a supplier with a good reputation who has the best capability to deliver steady
and high-quality service to Eva. This step is documented in our internal register.
There are a number of measures that are used in this step:
15

## Page 17

5.1.1 Non-disclosure agreement (NDA)
When selecting the supplier, it is possible that we need to share some company
information that is not publicly available. An NDA must be signed before doing so. Note
that such information is only shared when necessary.
5.1.2 Supplier comparison
When considering forming a relationship with a supplier for a certain purpose or project,
the Third Party owner compares at least two suppliers. The comparison is documented,
including (at least):
- A summary of the intended goal of the supplied product/service and a clear
description of supplied product/service;
- A list of requirements that the suppliers must meet as criteria to determine which
one is best aligned with bunq’s value suited for bunq and its goal of serving Eva. As
part of the selection requirement, the Third Party owner takes into account bunq’s
ESG approach, including but not limited to promote ESG-friendly investments,
respect fundamental human rights, and zero-tolerance to bribery and corruption;
and
- A conclusion of which supplier is selected and the reasoning behind the decision.
An outsourcing service provider needs to additionally fulfill the following requirements:
● Possess the necessary resources, experience, and qualifications to provide the outsourced activities in
an effective and competent manner;
● Possess all necessary licenses and permits;
● Have a good reputation;
● Have no (public) concerns about its financial stability and/or continuity.
Remote customer onboarding solution
When selecting a remote customer onboarding solution, the Third Party owner should
also assess the sufficiency, completeness, and accuracy of the data and documents to be
collected , as well as of the reliability and independence of the sources of information it
uses. This is important for the purposes of proper remote onboarding of users.
Personal data sharing
When personal data sharing is involved, suppliers originating in the EU/EEA must be
compliant with the General Data Protection Regulation, the leading EU-level data
protection law. Suppose the Third Party owner decides to select a new supplier that

originates from outside the EU/EEA and personal data will be shared. In that case, the

5.1.3 Proposal
For a relationship with a Third Party to be accepted within bunq, a proposal should be
created and submitted for working with a Third Party. If the supplier selection step is not
completed, the preliminary choice of the preferred supplier can be included.
The proposal should (at least) include the following:
● A clear description of activities the Third Party will supply, stating what Eva needs
and how that activity fulfills that need;
● The intended goal of the Third Party relationship and the associated evaluation
criteria, explaining why a supplier is needed;
● A clear plan on how to utilize the supplier.
In case of outsourcing, the following additional information should be included:
● An assessment whether the proposed outsourcing is material or not;
● A classification of the type of service model: IaaS, PaaS or SaaS (see p.9 of the DNB manual);
● A CIA rating of the proposed outsourcing.
5.1.4 Request for proposal (RFP)
There is an additional step in the Selection stage if it involves selection of a supplier with
an annual contract value larger than €500,000 should be preceded by a request for
proposal (RFP). The RFP is a request for a written proposal the supplier must submit to
create a relationship with bunq based on our requirements. The purpose of the RFP
process is to obtain more detailed information which enables the Third Party owner to
select well the Third Party that will engage with bunq. The RFP should clearly outline the
requirements of bunq and should be evaluated against the acceptance criteria
formulated by the stakeholders involved in the purpose of creation of a specific Third Party
relationship.
The Third Party owner is responsible for involving the Procurement department to ensure
an adequate RFP process is performed. Based on the RFP, a documented decision will be
taken, stating which supplier is selected and why.

Stage 2: Supplier Onboarding
The measures used in this stage ensure that working with the selected supplier will not
expose bunq to unnecessary or unwanted risks. The higher the supplier risk rating, the
stricter the controls. The following list of measures must be performed before onboarding
the supplier. Third Party owners should complete all controls and document them in our
internal register.
5.2.1 Supplier risk rating
The level of management imposed on the Third-party depends on the supplier risk rating.
The Third Party owner should determine the supplier risk rating as the first step in the
onboarding phase. The Third Party owner should inform the Third Parties Risk Manager of
the preliminary and final choice of the supplier, so the Risk Manager can provide them
with the supplier risk profile from a TPRM platform. Note that the European Supervisory
Authority shall designate the ICT third-party service providers that are critical for financial
entities.
The supplier risk rating is determined based on the following factors:
1. Service criticality
ICT service providers who do not provide outsourcing services nor services that
significantly affect the way bunq offers financial services to Eva, including Eva onboarding
and compliance management, and two-way communication with Eva are treated as low
risk and can be excluded from the consideration of the following factors.

## Page 20

2. Ownership
Ownership is the word for “responsibility” used in bunq. The importance of the supplied
product/service depends on the ownership it serves. The importance of the ownership is
based on the materiality of the ownership. The higher the inherent risk ranking of the
ownership, the more important it is.
3. Supplier risk profile
Contingent, the TPRM platform managed by the Risk Department, determines the
supplier’s risk profile based on their 1) financial stability, 2) ownership and control, and 3)
regulatory and compliance information. Contingent partners with ComplyAdvantage,
Acuris and Creditsafe for their sanctions and PEP screening services which has
international coverage, including the EU, US, and UK.
4. Switch cost
The ease with which a supplier could be replaced, i.e. whether the product/service is the
only one of its kind and whether it would take a lot of time and effort to switch to a
different supplier.
5. Commitment
The commitment bunq is making to a particular supplier is defined based on three risk
categories: Low, Medium, and High.
● Regulatory: The supplier is engaged as part of a regulatory requirement, and/or the
selection and evaluation of the supplier is subject to regulatory requirements. The
factor alone should be rated “Low”.
● One-off: Goods/services are acquired as individual transactions without
commitment for additional procurement. One-off transactions should be rated
“Low”. Repeat business with the same supplier can be considered a “one-off” if
there is no framework agreement or other relationship that implies follow-up
transactions.
● Recurring: Goods/services form part of a longer-term relationship. Recurring
transactions should be rated “Medium”. This can include subscriptions that are
automatically extended (subscriptions that are automatically canceled after the
expiry of the first term and last no more than one year can be considered one-off

transactions), service contracts, or other situations where a contract is signed
which indicates a longer-term relationship.
● Outsourcing: When the supplier is classified as an outsourcing service provider
(section 3), this factor should be rated at least “Medium”.
● Contract value: Monetary value of the goods/services, calculated over the contract

5.2.2 Due diligence
Due diligence, in the context of this policy, is the process of assessing and verifying the

supplier, to identify any potential risks and mitigants. The Third Party owner can obtain
information needed to complete the relevant risk assessments from TPRM Platform and
by sending the supplier the due diligence questionnaire.

It mainly concerns Eva’s wishes related to the continuity of bunq’s license, the fairness of

● Financial crime risk;
● Regulatory compliance risk;
● Conflict of interest risk;
● Environmental risk;
● Corporate social responsibility risk.

● IT resilience risk;
● IT security risk;
● Data management risk;
● Risk management risk;
● Process resilience risk;

5.2.3 Data Protection Compliance
1. Record of processing activities (RPA)
If the supplier processes personal data, the Third Party owner must inform bunq’s Data
Protection Officer to update the record of processing activities (RPA). The controller or
processor is required to maintain the RPA as part of its responsibility to comply with the
GDPR. The record should be made available to the relevant supervisory authority upon
request.
2. Data protection impact assessment (DPIA)
In case the supplier processes personal data, bunq, as the controller, must determine if a
DPIA should be performed. The Third Party owner must assess if such processing is “likely
to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons”. If it is likely to do
24

## Page 26

that, the Third Party owner is required to conduct a Data Protection Impact Assessment. It
enables the Third Party owner to identify potential privacy risks and take steps to mitigate
them, managing the privacy risks. The Third Party owner must request the advice of the
DPO in such cases.
A single assessment may address a set of similar processing operations with similar high
risks when the nature, scope, context, and purposes of the processing operations are very
similar to the processing for which DPIAs have already been carried out. In such cases, it is
not mandatory to perform a new DPIA, and the results of DPIA for similar processing can
be used.
However, the Third Party owner must always monitor if the data processing changes. If so,
the Third Party owner must always check whether the new data processing requires the
execution of a new DPIA. Even if there is no new data processing or the new one does not
require a DPIA, the Third Party owner should review and adjust the DPIA periodically.
2.1 Prior consultation
Third Party owner shall consult the supervisory authority before the beginning of
processing where a DPIA indicates that there is a high residual risk (i.e. risk remains high
even after mitigation measures or if there is no mitigation possible). Third Party owner can
request a prior consultation with the Dutch Data Protection Authority (Autoriteit
Persoonsgegevens).

● Availability of service, i.e. the amount of time the service is available for use;
● Defect rates, i.e. counts or percentages of errors in major criteria (e.g. quality
control tests results, risk incident counts, coding errors/re-works, missed deadlines);
● Business results, i.e. customer satisfaction levels, complaints, support waiting
times, amount of time for problem resolution etc.;

Conflicts of interest
A conflict of interest arises where two or more competing interests are present and where
acting in the interests of one of the parties would or could mean acting contrary to the
interests of the other party or parties.
The outsourcing arrangement should not create a conflict of interest between the service
provider, Eva and/or bunq, including its branches and subsidiaries. The Third Party owner
should identify and assess if an outsourcing arrangement creates material or potential
conflicts of interest. Appropriate measures must be taken to manage those conflicts of
interest in compliance with this Policy and bunq’s Conflict of Interest Policy.

and the owner is required to conduct a data protection impact assessment (DPIA).
