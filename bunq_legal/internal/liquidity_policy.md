2. Liquidity risk
Liquidity risk is the risk that a bank may not be able to meet its obligations to provide cash
to a withdrawing user in a timely manner or will do so at an excessive cost to itself. This
risk may arise if customers suddenly withdraw more funds than expected and/or the
amount withdrawn is higher than the amount bunq holds in its reserves. To reduce
liquidity risk, bunq holds assets that are liquid in nature. A liquid asset has key
characteristics including the ability to sell the asset quickly and in size, close to the market
value.
Liquidity risk can be further categorized into two risks:
● Funding liquidity risk, which refers to the inability of the bank to meet its
obligations to its users because of insufficient cash available.
● Market liquidity risk, which arises when the bank cannot convert its liquidity buffer
into cash without incurring excessive costs to itself.

2.1 Liquidity risk drivers
There are several risk drivers for liquidity risk that have been identified as impacting bunq,
such as:
● Retail funding risk:
○ the risk of unexpected large fund transfers by user persons and (small) user
companies.
● Wholesale funding risk:
○ the risk that arises from bunq’s dependence on transfers of funds from
other financial institutions and credit providers to bunq.
● Intraday liquidity risk:
○ bunq is exposed to this risk due to different times of cash payments and
settlements during the day. These settlements are daily (MasterCard),
several times during the day (e.g. Swift) and continuous (instant payments).
● Liquidity risk of marketable assets:
○ refers to the risk of an unexpected loss of liquidity in and value of tradable
assets, such as bonds.
● Funding concentration risk and market accessibility:
○ refers to the risk that bunq depends on a limited number of funding
sources and it could be difficult to access other sources on short notice if it
becomes necessary.
● Liquidity risk associated with off-balance obligations:
○ refers to the risk related to derivative securities and committed credit to
third parties. Not performing our contractual and non-contractual
commitments that are not shown on the balance sheet could lead to
financial and reputational damage for bunq.
● Liquidity risk caused by reputation risk, business model, general strategy, and IT
stability:

○ This risk can be caused by a loss of confidence of users in bunq. This loss of
confidence may be triggered by IT disruptions, bad press and rumors, and
lack of trust in bunq’s future.
● Liquidity risk connected to foreign exchange of currency and related instruments:
○ The risk of unexpected disruptions in the foreign exchange (“FX”) markets,
which could result in illiquidity in the FX market and impact bunq.
● Payments supporting the committed obligations:
○ bunq commits to provide future funds for mortgage origination and to

3. Governance
The Managing Board is responsible for the adoption, implementation, monitoring, and –
when necessary – adjustment of this policy. The liquidity policy and any adjustments are
approved by the Managing Board. Responsibilities and roles within bunq are organized
using a Three Lines of Defence Model, which is further explained in the Risk Management
Policy. In terms of liquidity risk, the responsibility for accepting and managing such risks
lies with the First line of defense, specifically the Treasury Department. The rationale for
such allocation of responsibility is that those who are responsible for taking risks are also
accountable for managing those risks, as expressed in bunq’s Risk Appetite Statement
(RAS). The second line of defense is the Risk Department led by the Head of Risk, who is

On a monthly basis, bunq’s liquidity metrics and funding situation are presented to and

can withstand periods of financial stress. If bunq’s liquidity is insufficient to meet bunq’s
obligations then the Exit Plan is triggered.
4. Liquidity Risk Management
There are various elements to bunq’s Liquidity Management framework.
1. The first element for mitigating liquidity risk is the Primary Liquidity Buffer, which
is composed of high-quality liquid assets.
2. The second element is the Secondary Liquidity Buffer.
3. The third element is a set of Limits and Thresholds for the Main Liquidity Metrics.
4. The fourth element is a set of Strategies for Effective Liquidity Management.
These elements are explained in more detail below.
4.1 Limits and Thresholds for the Main Liquidity Metrics
To manage liquidity risk, bunq has established thresholds for the most important liquidity
benchmarks. These thresholds are listed below:
Risk limits that are defined within bunq’s RAS:
a. Regulatory reserve requirements: the amount of funds that a bank holds in its ECB
reserve to ensure that it is able to respond to sudden withdrawals by its
customers/meet liabilities in case of sudden withdrawals.
b. Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR): the LCR ensures that a bank holds sufficient
High-Quality Liquid Assets (HQLA) to survive a 30-day period of liquidity stress.

c. Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR): this ratio requires banks to hold enough stable
funding to cover the repayment duration of their long-term assets.
d. Asset Encumbrance: Assets that creditors can take possession of if the bank fails to
meet its commitments. If a borrower defaults, the lender can liquidate the asset to
recover their cash.
e. Survival period: is defined as the number of months a bank can continue to
operate using available cash and collateral without access to funding markets. This
is determined by applying bunq’s liquidity stress scenarios as defined in the
Liquidity Stress Testing Methodology paper.
f. Liquidity gap: to ensure we don’t fund gaps between the expected maturity of our
stable non-maturing deposits and the maturity of our assets we have run a
liquidity gap analysis which results in limits to the maturity profile of our assets.
Other liquidity restrictions:
g. TARGET2 and EBA Clearing: these are external payment settlement systems in the
Eurozone that allow EU banks to transfer funds between one another. We set
desired liquidity ranges for these accounts in the Appendix to the liquidity policy -
External Settlement Account Management final.
h. Concentrated positions: occur when a small number of customers deposit a
substantial amount at bunq, representing a material portion of funds for bunq. A
concentrated position may also arise when a bank has a material share of its
lending portfolio concentrated in a single counterparty. Each month we monitor
the largest deposits at bunq within the ALCO.
i. Intercompany liquidity
Limits: We maintain a limit on Intercompany lending between bunq BV and CFG as this

4.3 High Quality Liquid Assets
For any asset to be treated as HQLA eligible certain operational requirements have to be
met in line with LCR DR Article 8. In this section we discuss the policies and processes
followed to ensure compliance.
Diversification: the HQLA asset base needs to be sufficiently diverse. To achieve this
diversification we apply the following:
ABS: We set an individual exposure limit within our RAS for ABS notes. This limit
guarantees that our exposure to a single HQLA-eligible ABS note remains significantly
smaller than the minimum tranche size of €250 million required by LCR DR Article 12 (1a)

5. Measuring Liquidity Risk
bunq performs liquidity risk monitoring and stress tests to measure and understand the
impact different negative scenarios can have on the bank's liquidity. This section describes
the strategies taken by bunq to measure liquidity risk.
5.1 Liquidity risk monitoring process
For effective liquidity management and measuring, bunq established a liquidity risk
monitoring process. This monitoring process includes various internal and external
liquidity reports that provide an overview of bunq’s liquidity buffer, regulatory liquidity
ratios, and the available liquidity to invest. A detailed description of the different reports
that compose the liquidity risk monitoring process as well as the current treatment for
deposits can be found below.
a. Liquidity risk reporting
● Daily cash reporting: Between 09:00 - 10:00 and 16:30 - 18:00 the cash balances on
RTGS, TIPS, RT1, CGS, ECB deposit facility, and the ECB credit facility are reported by
the Finance department. Any breach of pre-defined limits will result in a group
notification.
● Daily deposit reporting: On a daily basis Looker data is automatically sent to the
Treasury and Finance team via email with daily deposit balances. Additionally, a
historical breakdown is provided.
● Weekly liquidity reports: Treasury receives a report every week with subsidiaries'
forecasted funding requirements.
● Ongoing LCR and NSFR forecasting: within Lucanet we calculate the HQLA
balance, NSFR, and LCR ratios (consolidated and solo). These calculations are
applied to actual financials and the budget to determine the impact of future
expected balance sheet changes.

5.2 Stress-testing
Stress testing is a helpful risk management tool that provides insight into whether bunq
maintains adequate liquidity levels in times of stress. Therefore, liquidity risk is managed
by running different scenarios that pertain to the risk control framework. The scenarios are
translated into liquidity inflows and liquidity outflows and can also have an impact on the
value of liquid assets.
Stress tests are reviewed, together with the internal liquidity adequacy assessment
process (ILAAP), annually. The Head of Treasury assesses the relevance of the scenarios
and whether they reflect the liquidity risk profile of bunq. Also, the parameters and
completeness of the scenarios are assessed. The CRO will perform a review of the
proposed scenarios which will then be used in the ILAAP. The CRO presents the stress
tests to the Management Board.
Scenario descriptions are provided in the Liquidity Stress Testing Methodology document.
5.3 Liquidity gap analysis
To ensure bunq has enough money on hand and doesn't invest too much in long-term
assets like mortgages, we check our financial situation every month using a method
called a Liquidity Gap Analysis. This Liquidity Gap Analysis is a tool that helps bunq
understand the difference between our available cash and the financial obligations we
have over specific periods.
Here's how the analysis works:
● Time Buckets. The analysis breaks down bunq's future financial timeline into
specific "time buckets": 0-1 days, 1-30 days, 1-3 months, 3-6 months, 6-12 months, 1-2
years, 2-5 years, 5-7 years, 7-10 years and 10-100 years. Within each of these time
17

## Page 19

buckets, we calculate the money coming in and going out from all of bunq's assets
and liabilities, except the interest payments.
● Scenario and general assumptions. The analysis is based on a scenario where bunq
faces minor cash-related difficulties. Specifically:
○ New Deposits: There's an assumption that no new deposits are coming in
over the next 100 years.
○ Cash flows: Outflows and inflows for the principal repayments are taken at
100%, no prepayments or option executions are assumed . The contractual
principal cash flows are calculated from bunq’s ALM model for financial
assets. Principal repayments are not reinvested and are attributed to the
time buckets based on the payment dates.
○ Deposit Outflows: There's an immediate (within 1 day after the valuation
date, so it falls into time bucket “0-1 days”) moderate outflow of "unstable"6
deposits. Additionally, over the next 30 years depositors withdraw their
money from bunq at a rate called “decay rate”7. Basically, the scenario
assumes that all deposits will be withdrawn from bunq during the next 30
years.
○ Secondary liquidity buffer: The scenario allows for the application of the
Secondary liquidity buffer, even though it wouldn't typically be used in such
minor challenges. This is to balance out the very negative assumption
about the lack of new deposits. The Secondary buffer mostly consists of the
RMBS notes that can be used as collateral. For more details on the
Secondary liquidity buffer refer to Section 4.5.
The value of the RMBS notes for the purpose of using them as collateral is evaluated by
DNB on a daily basis. The notional of these notes is amortized over time, however, this isn’t
taken into account as the principal repayments will be held in cash.
Limits on investments: Any excess money (liquidity surplus) in a time bucket indicates the
amount bunq can allocate to investments that will return funds at a point in future within
the corresponding time bucket. This allows bunq to make investment decisions without
risking a cash shortage in the future.
Both the Stress-testing and the Liquidity gap analysis define the limits for investments
into future time buckets, and the stricter limits are always applied.
